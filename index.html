🔬 Scanning Ion Conductance Microscopy

A powerful non-contact imaging technique for studying live cells, soft materials, and surface charge distribution at nanometer resolution.

📐 What is SICM? ▼

SICM uses a nanopipette filled with electrolyte solution as a scanning probe. The ionic current is sensitive to tip-sample distance.

  • Principle: Ion current through nanopipette aperture decreases as tip approaches surface
  • Non-contact: No mechanical force applied to sample
  • Resolution: Determined by pipette inner radius (ri = 10-100 nm)
  • Environment: Works in physiological buffer solutions
  • Applications: Live cell imaging, membrane dynamics, ion channel mapping

Current-Distance Relationship:

At large distances, current I₀ is constant (bulk). As tip approaches within ~1 ri of surface, current decreases due to access resistance increase. Setpoint (97-99% of I₀) defines imaging height.

Imaging Modes:

• DC mode: Continuous current feedback
• Hopping mode: Approach-retract at each pixel (better for rough samples)
• ICM: Ion conductance microscopy with modulated distance

⚡ QSCM Method ▼

Quantitative Surface Charge Microscopy maps local surface charge density by measuring height differences at dual bias potentials.

  • Dual-bias: Apply +100mV and -100mV to pipette
  • Approach curves: Record I vs distance at each bias
  • Δh extraction: Height difference at constant setpoint
  • Calibration: Convert Δh to σ using known references

Physical Basis:

Surface charges create electric double layer (EDL). Positive pipette bias (+100mV) attracts anions, negative bias attracts cations. On negatively charged surface, +100mV allows closer approach → Δh < 0.

Calibration (Klausen 2016):

σ = (Δh - 0.81) × 5.79 mC/m²
Valid for: ri ≈ 15nm, 150mM NaCl, ±100mV, 99% setpoint

Reference Materials:

• Mica: σ ≈ -34 mC/m² (highly negative)
• APTES: σ ≈ 0 (neutral)
• SiO₂: σ ≈ -16 mC/m²

🔍 Interpretation & Reference Comparison

📚 Theory & Methodology

1. Scanning Ion Conductance Microscopy (SICM)

SICM uses a glass nanopipette filled with electrolyte solution as a non-contact probe. A bias voltage (typically ±100 mV) drives ionic current through the pipette opening. As the tip approaches a surface, ion flow becomes restricted — the access resistance increases — and the measured current drops. This current drop is used as a distance signal, keeping the tip at a fixed height above the sample without ever touching it.

(1)
I(z) = Vbias / (Rpip + Raccess(z))
Total current = bias divided by pipette resistance + access resistance. Raccess increases as z → 0 (tip near surface), causing I to drop.

The access resistance scales approximately as Raccess ∝ 1/z for a flat surface. In practice, the current drops to ~99% of its bulk value at a tip–surface distance equal to roughly the inner radius of the pipette (ri). This defines the lateral and axial resolution: a smaller pipette gives sharper images but lower signal.

Example: A pipette with ri = 50 nm in 150 mM NaCl at +100 mV typically gives Ibulk ≈ 1–3 nA. When the tip is 50 nm from a glass surface, current drops to ~98–99% of bulk (the 1% setpoint).

2. Approach Curve Processing

Raw approach curves (distance vs current) are normalized to the bulk current. This removes slow drift in the baseline and allows direct comparison across different tips, days, or bias polarities.

(2)
Ibulk(z) = a·z + b    (linear fit over far-field, last 50% of curve)
Baseline estimated by linear regression on the flat region far from the surface, where the tip does not yet feel the sample.
(3)
Inorm(z) = 100 × |I(z)| / Ibulk(z)
Normalized current expressed as % of bulk. At large z → 100%. At surface → drops to setpoint (e.g. 99%).

Why normalize? Without normalization, a tip with Rpip = 100 MΩ gives different raw currents than one with 200 MΩ, even over the same sample. Normalization makes all curves start at 100% regardless of tip resistance.

Setpoint choice: The x-value (distance) is extracted at a chosen setpoint, e.g. 99%. A stricter setpoint (e.g. 99.5%) gives a position closer to the surface but is more sensitive to noise. The default 99% is a good compromise for most SICM experiments.

3. Trace & Retrace

In a single spectroscopy acquisition, the piezo first moves the tip toward the surface (approach = trace) and then retracts it (retrace). Ideally these two halves should be identical. Differences reveal:

  • Tip contamination: The tip picks up material on approach, which partially blocks the opening. Current on retrace is slightly lower than on trace at the same distance.
  • Sample deformation: Soft samples (lipid bilayers, cells) compress slightly under the electrostatic or hydrodynamic force of the approaching tip. The surface appears "closer" on approach than on retrace.
  • Slow equilibration: If the approach speed is faster than the ion redistribution time at the surface (relevant for charged surfaces in low-salt buffers), hysteresis appears even without physical contact.

Practical rule: If trace and retrace Δh values agree within ± 1 nm, the measurement is reliable. A systematic offset of > 2 nm suggests the approach speed should be reduced or the tip replaced.

4. Δh & Surface Charge (QSCM)

When two approach curves are recorded at opposite bias polarities (+V and −V) over the same position, the curves are identical for an uncharged surface. On a charged surface, the electric double layer shifts the effective tip–surface distance differently for each polarity. This shift, Δh, is the core QSCM observable:

(4)
Δh = x2(−V) − x1(+V)
Distance at setpoint for −V bias minus distance at setpoint for +V bias. Units: nm. Δh > 0 for negative surfaces (anions repel Cl⁻ carrier ions, reducing current faster at +V).

Physical picture: At +100 mV bias, Cl⁻ ions carry most of the current from pipette to bath. Near a negatively charged surface, the local anion concentration is depleted (repulsion), so the current drops earlier — at a larger apparent distance. At −100 mV, Na⁺ ions dominate; a negative surface attracts them and delays the current drop. The difference in these two distances is Δh.

Example values: A bare glass surface in 150 mM NaCl typically gives Δh ≈ −2 to −4 nm (negative charge). A lipid bilayer of DPPE (near-neutral) gives Δh ≈ 0–1 nm. DPPG (negatively charged headgroup) gives Δh ≈ −5 to −8 nm. An amine-functionalized surface (APTES, positive charge) gives Δh > 0.

(5)
σ = m × (Δh − c)
Linear calibration converts Δh (nm) to surface charge density σ (mC/m²). Default: m = 5.79 mC/m²/nm, c = 0.81 nm (from Klausen et al. 2016, ri = 50 nm, 150 mM NaCl).

Calibration validity: The m and c constants depend on pipette geometry (ri, ro/ri), electrolyte concentration, and bias magnitude. The default values apply specifically to ri = 50 nm pipettes in 150 mM NaCl at ±100 mV. For different conditions, recalibrate against surfaces of known charge density.

5. Super-Resolution SR-SICM

Lateral resolution in SICM is fundamentally limited by the pipette inner radius ri. The point spread function (PSF) — how the pipette "sees" a sharp feature — is approximately a disc of radius ri. Structures smaller than ri appear blurred in the raw image.

Richardson–Lucy (RL) deconvolution iteratively sharpens the image by dividing out the PSF contribution. Starting from the measured image g and a PSF model h, the algorithm refines the estimate f of the true sample:

(6)
f(k+1) = f(k) · (hT ∗ [g / (h ∗ f(k))])
RL iteration. hT = mirror-flipped PSF. ∗ = convolution with mirror boundary conditions. Converges in 5–50 iterations for typical SICM images.

PSF model: The PSF is computed analytically from pipette geometry — inner radius ri, outer-to-inner ratio ro/ri, and the half-cone angle α. A larger ro/ri (thicker glass wall) gives a more extended PSF and stronger blurring. The PSF size in pixels is set to cover ≥ 3× the pipette radius relative to the scan field.

Example: A 256×256 pixel scan of a 1 µm × 1 µm area with a ri = 50 nm pipette: features down to ~20 nm can be resolved after 10–20 RL iterations. After deconvolution, membrane protein clusters (typical diameter 20–100 nm) become clearly resolvable from the lipid bilayer background.

Iteration choice: Too few iterations (< 5) under-sharpen; too many (> 50) amplify noise into ring artifacts around sharp edges. For noisy images, 5–15 iterations is typical; for high SNR images, 20–30 is appropriate.

6. Statistics for Batch Δh Measurements

When measuring Δh at multiple spatial positions (e.g. a grid of 10 points across a cell membrane), the batch gives a distribution of Δh values reflecting true heterogeneity plus measurement uncertainty.

(7)
SEM = SD / √n   |   CI95% = x̄ ± tα/2, n−1 × SEM
SEM = standard error of the mean. CI uses the Student t-distribution with n−1 degrees of freedom. For n < 10 the CI is substantially wider than for n > 30.

Which uncertainty to report? SD describes the spread of individual measurements (biological variability + noise). SEM describes confidence in the mean. For comparing two conditions, use SEM or CI. For characterising heterogeneity, use SD. For n < 5, report individual points alongside the mean.

One-sample t-test (Δh ≠ 0): Tests whether the measured Δh is significantly different from zero (i.e. from an uncharged surface). A p-value < 0.05 indicates the surface is detectably charged under these conditions. This is the most common test for QSCM experiments on a single surface type.

Paired t-test (x₂ − x₁): Directly tests whether the individual Δh differences across all points are non-zero. More sensitive than the one-sample test when x₁ and x₂ are correlated (same point measured at both biases). Use this when you have matched +V/−V pairs at each spatial position.

How to manage users

Open the HTML file in VS Code and find the LAB_USERS array (search for it with Ctrl+F).

Each user entry looks like this:

{ username: 'ali', password: 'ali123', displayName: 'Ali', role: 'PhD Student', isAdmin: false }

To add a user: copy one line, change the values, add a comma.

To remove a user: delete the line.

To make someone admin: set isAdmin: true.

⚠️ Passwords are stored in plain text in the HTML — this is a local tool, not a web server.
